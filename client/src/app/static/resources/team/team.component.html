Human Phenotype Ontology Team

The Human Phenotype Ontology (HPO) has been under development since 2008 and has benefited enormously from a team-science approach under the auspices of the Monarch Initiative, establish in 2009. From the beginning, the HPO has worked with clinicians to improve, refine, and extend areas of the HPO to meet the needs of clinicians and translational researchers. The first such workshop was conducted in 2009 at the Wellcome Trust Sanger Institute with Professors Helen Firth and Nigel Carter. OMIM

The initial version of the HPO was constructed in 2008 using a file from the Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man (OMIM) resource by extracting the textual descriptions of each disease as listed in OMIM’s Clinical Synopsis section. The HPO was constructed using in-house scripts and Java programs as well as the ontology editing tool OBO Edit by manually curating all items that occured twice or more in the entire OMIM corpus. We merged synonyms into unique HPO terms. For instance, the three OMIM descriptions Carpal bone hypoplasia, Hypoplasia of carpal bones, and Hypoplastic carpal bones were fused into the single term HP:0001498, Carpal bone hypoplasia. Domain knowledge was also used to develop a subclass hierarchy and to define the necessary general terms, such as Aplasia/hypoplasia of the outer ear, to group more specific terms, as well as to define links between individual terms. Details about the initial construction of the HPO can be found in the initial publication. We would like to acknowledge countless constructive interactions with the OMIM team since 2008.

Orphanet

The HPO project is collaborating with Orphanet to create an interoperable suite of ontology resources for rare disease based on the HPO and the Orphanet Rare Disease Ontology (ORDO). Orphanet, coordinated by INSERM US14, maintains the Orphanet Nomenclature of Rare Diseases and Orphanet Rare Disease Ontology (ORDO). The HPO project is collaborating with Orphanet to create an interoperable suite of ontology resources for rare disease based on the HPO and the Orphanet Rare Disease Ontology (ORDO). One of these resources is HOOM, the HPO-ORDO Ontological Module: this module qualifies the annotation between a clinical entity and phenotypic abnormalities according to a frequency and by integrating the notion of diagnostic criterion.

The Phenotype and Trait Ontology Project

The teams in Berlin and in the UK and US first joined forces in 2009 under the auspices of an NIH-funded project that we call the “PATO grant” (2009-2014). Here we laid the foundation for the computational logic that underlies the HPO, Mammalian Phenotype Ontology, and zebrafish phenotype annotations by developing a scheme for creating logical definitions of individual phenotypic abnormalities. The framework is based on a number of computational resources include the Phenotype and Trait Ontology (PATO) and the Uberon cross-species anatomy ontology. This framework allows not only computational quality control of the ontologies and annotations but also enables cross-species phenotypic comparisons.

IRDiRC

The International Rare DIseases Research Consortium aims to enable all people living with a rare disease to receive an accurate diagnosis, care, and available therapy within one year of coming to medical attention. The HPO utilized by almost all member countries and has been a key source of collaboration, validation, and community dissemination. HPO has therefore been adopted as a Recommended Resource for use in rare disease diagnostics by the IRDiRC.
