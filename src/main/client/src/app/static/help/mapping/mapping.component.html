Translations

The HPO has been translated into many languages including (in alphabetical order) Chinese, Dutch, French, German, Italian, Japanese, Portugese, Russion, Spanish, and Turkish. Some of the translations can be downloaded from the HPO Translation webpage. Information about the Japanese translation is available on the HPO Japanese GitHub page. The Chinese HPO project offers a Wiki page.

Lyfe Languages Project

Lyfe Languages commenced during the 2019 United Nations International Year of Indigenous Languages as part of Project Y. It involves the translation of Human Phenotype Ontology (HPO) terms into Indigenous languages, including Australian Aboriginal & Torres Strait Islander languages in Australia and Indigenous Ghanaian Languages. These translations will enable a more effective and personal approach in communication between health care providers and patients. The complexity of HPO can be lost on most people, therefore translation into language will improve patient understanding of what they’re experiencing.

Translations are being completed in partnership between Champions, Elders, community, community medical centres, local language centres and local land councils. This approach strengthens bonds between all the groups involved, ensuring all voices are heard and that community empowerment is achieved.

Currently there are several Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander languages across Australia being translated including:

  • Nyangumarta
  • Noongar
  • Badimaya
  • Eastern Arrernte
  • Kija
  • Kala Lagaw Ya and Creol
  • Manyjilyjarra

There are over 290 Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander language groups across Australia. On average, 1 in 10 families speak an Indigenous language at home. In regional parts of Australia, that figure can jump up to 5.8 in 10 families. People living in regional parts of Australia also experience a much more different health journey than those living in metropolitan areas, which includes limited access to primary and specialist health care, large distances to travel, referral to hospital specialists often involving travelling to larger metropolitan hospitals usually by way of airplane, and the list goes on.

Examples of translations completed in Nyangumarta include:

HPO Term – Microcephaly
  • Description – Small head circumference
  • Translation – Jurnturtu wupartu
HPO Term – Macrocephaly
  • Description – Big head circumference
  • Translation – Jurnturtu wirtu
HPO Term – Hypertelorism
  • Description – widely spaced eyes
  • Translation – Pani kakajul
HPO Term – Macrostomia
  • Description – wide mouth
  • Translation – Jawa walpurra

Mappings

In contrast to many clinical terminologies, the HPO was purpose-built to facilitate differential diagnostics and scientific investigations such as cross-species comparisons with model organism data from mouse and zebrafish. Moreover, the HPO has an extremely broad coverage of concepts. In an evaluation of HPO content versus the numerous vocabularies integrated within the Unified Medical Language System (UMLS), Winnenburg and Bodenreider showed that the coverage of HPO phenotype concepts in the UMLS is 54% and only 30% in SNOMED CT (ISMB 2014 SIG session cited here). The UMLS is a terminology integration system developed by the U.S. National Library of Medicine that integrates many standard biomedical terminologies. In order to improve the coverage of phenotype data, the UMLS has now integrated the entire HPO starting with the 2015AB release. This enables an easy process to map HPO-encoded data to standard health-care terminologies such as SNOMED CT.

HPO has contributed to the establishment of the International Consortium of Human Phenotype Terminologies (ICHPT) to provide the community with standards that achieve interoperability among databases incorporating human phenotypic features.
